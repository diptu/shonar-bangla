# ⚡ Energy Security

> *An economy that's growing can't run on imported fuel and load-shedding forever.*

![Timeline](https://img.shields.io/badge/timeline-2026--2036-006A4E?style=flat-square)
![Status](https://img.shields.io/badge/status-proposed-F42A1D?style=flat-square)

---

A small workshop owner in Narayanganj has learned to schedule his machines around the power cuts, not the other way around. Bangladesh imports much of the fuel that keeps its lights on and its factories running — a dependency that shows up as both a monthly import bill and, some afternoons, as no power at all. Growth needs energy it can count on.

This is the story of building that certainty.

## Chapter I — Stopping the Leaks (Years 1–3)

Grid modernization cuts transmission losses that have quietly wasted power for years. Rooftop and utility-scale solar get real incentives — Bangladesh has the sun; it's mostly lacked the policy. LNG import terminals fill the near-term gap while renewables scale up behind them.

## Chapter II — Shifting the Mix (Years 4–7)

Renewable capacity — solar, and wind along the coast — starts covering a meaningful share of new demand growth, not just headlines. Regional power-sharing agreements with India and Nepal add a buffer that reduces how much any single shortfall can hurt. Industrial energy efficiency standards tighten, so growth stops meaning proportional demand growth.

## Chapter III — Power That Doesn't Flicker (Years 8–10)

By year ten, renewables make up a serious share of the energy mix, import dependency has meaningfully eased, and load-shedding is the exception, not the seasonal norm. The workshop owner in Narayanganj plans his day around his orders again, not the grid.

## The Obstacles

Renewable buildout competes for land in one of the world's most densely populated countries. Grid modernization is capital-intensive and slow to show results. And regional power agreements depend on relationships that can shift with politics.

## The Numbers That Tell Us It's Working

- Renewable share of energy mix, rising
- Transmission and distribution losses, falling
- Load-shedding hours per year, falling
