# 🎓 Education & Human Capital

> *High enrollment isn't the problem. Weak learning outcomes are.*

A student in Rajshahi has near-perfect attendance and a stack of memorized answers — and no clear idea how to apply any of it to a real job. She's not the exception. Bangladesh got the hard part right: kids are in classrooms. What happens inside those classrooms is the part still being written.

This is the story of turning enrollment into ability.

## Chapter I — Fixing the Foundation (Years 1–3)

It starts with teachers — better trained, better paid, given room to teach beyond rote memorization. Assessment shifts from *what did you memorize* to *what can you actually do*. Slow, unglamorous, essential work.

## Chapter II — Pointing Toward the Future (Years 4–7)

Vocational tracks appear at the secondary level, built around where the economy is actually headed: renewables, logistics, digital trades. A student choosing this path isn't settling for "less than university" — she's choosing a direct line to employment that didn't used to exist.

## Chapter III — Competing on the World Stage (Years 8–10)

Functional English and digital literacy become the norm, not the privilege of a few English-medium schools. Research funding builds two or three universities that belong in global rankings, giving Bangladesh's best minds a reason to build their careers at home. The student from Rajshahi, ten years on, has options her older siblings didn't.

## The Obstacles

Teachers leave for better-paying work if retention isn't taken seriously. Curriculum reform meets resistance from systems built around the old way. And research universities need funding that outlasts a single budget cycle.

## The Numbers That Tell Us It's Working

- Learning-adjusted years of schooling, rising
- Graduate employability rate, rising
