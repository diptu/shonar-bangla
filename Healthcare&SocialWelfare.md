# 🩺 Healthcare & Social Welfare

> *High out-of-pocket costs. Thin rural access. A system stretched by informal-sector risk.*

A rickshaw puller in Sylhet gets sick for two weeks and his family's savings disappear with him. There's no insurance behind him — most of what he'd pay for care comes straight out of his own pocket, the same way it does for millions of informal workers across the country. One illness, one injury, and a family that was stable slides toward poverty.

This is the story of putting something underneath them.

## Chapter I — Reaching the Last Mile (Years 1–3)

Community clinics expand, linked by telemedicine to district hospitals — so a rural health worker isn't guessing alone when a case gets serious. Care starts closer to where people actually live.

## Chapter II — A Safety Net With a Name (Years 4–7)

A national health insurance pilot launches for informal-sector workers — garment labor, agriculture, gig work — the people who've historically had none. It's a pilot, not a promise, and it's designed to be scaled once it proves out.

## Chapter III — Coverage That Doesn't Ask Who You Are (Years 8–10)

Universal basic health coverage becomes real, not aspirational. Maternal mortality and childhood stunting rates close in on the best in the region. The rickshaw puller from year one, if he gets sick again, doesn't lose everything to it this time.

## The Obstacles

Insurance schemes fail if the financing model isn't sustainable. Rural clinics fail if they can't keep staff. And telemedicine only works where the connection — and the trust in it — actually holds.
